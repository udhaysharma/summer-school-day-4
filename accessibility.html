10 Accessibility Mistakes Everyone Makes

Published May 28, 2023 • 8 min read

From poor contrast ratios to missing alt text, most websites have accessibility issues. Learn the most common pitfalls and how to fix them to make your sites usable by everyone...Accessibility in web design ensures that websites and applications are usable by people with disabilities, including visual, auditory, motor, and cognitive impairments. Despite growing awareness, many developers and designers still make common accessibility mistakes that significantly hinder user experience for millions of people. One of the most frequent mistakes is the lack of **alternative text for images**. When images are not described using the `alt` attribute, screen readers cannot convey the image’s purpose to visually impaired users, leaving them without critical content or context. Similarly, **poor color contrast** is a widespread issue. Many designers choose aesthetic color combinations that look good visually but fail to provide enough contrast between text and background, making content difficult or impossible to read for users with low vision or color blindness. for everything, it creates confusion and reduces the usability of assistive technologies. Additionally, **missing or improper use of labels in forms** can make form inputs difficult to navigate. Screen readers rely on labels to explain the purpose of each input field. Without these labels, users might not know what information to enter, especially if visual cues are not accessible. **Keyboard accessibility** is another common area where mistakes happen. Many developers design interfaces that rely heavily on mouse interactions, ignoring the fact that some users navigate entirely using a keyboard or other assistive devices. Elements such as buttons, links, and form fields must be reachable and operable using the Tab key, and visual indicators (focus styles) must clearly show which element is active. Failing to include proper **focus management** can leave users “lost” in the interface. Similarly, **inaccessible pop-ups and modal dialogs** are a frequent barrier. If focus doesn’t shift properly when a modal opens or if the modal can’t be dismissed via keyboard, users may be trapped. One often overlooked mistake is **missing skip navigation links**. These links allow keyboard users to bypass repetitive content, like menus, and jump directly to the main content. Without them, users have to tab through every menu item on every page, leading to frustration. Additionally, **relying solely on visual cues** such as color to convey information is problematic. For example, marking errors in red without an accompanying icon or text label will confuse users with color blindness. Finally, a critical and common oversight is **not testing with real assistive technologies**. Developers might assume their site is accessible because it “looks good” or passes automated tests, but true accessibility can only be verified through manual testing with screen readers, keyboards, and tools like VoiceOver or NVDA. Ignoring accessibility doesn’t just affect users—it can lead to legal consequences under accessibility laws like the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) or Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG). In short, by recognizing and correcting these common accessibility mistakes, designers and developers can create more inclusive, user-friendly experiences for everyone, regardless of their abilities.
